*[Components A (Microfluidic Chip) and B (Flow Management) — to be completed by hardware team]*

**Component C — Machine Learning Classification Engine (Computation Team — James)**

The third component of IMULAS is an onboard machine learning engine that receives processed biomarker concentrations from the microfluidic sensing array and produces real-time health classifications for crew review. The ML engine runs locally on an embedded processor integrated with the IMULAS hardware, eliminating dependence on ground-based data links and enabling autonomous health monitoring during communication blackouts or deep-space missions.

The system receives a feature vector of analyte concentrations (e.g., calcium, creatinine, oxalate, citrate, pH) from Component B's signal processing stage. Each sample is normalized against the individual astronaut's pre-flight baseline to account for person-to-person physiological variation. The model then classifies the sample as nominal or anomalous, providing flight surgeons with an immediate health status indicator.

Development follows an incremental architecture strategy. The initial prototype uses a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) for binary health classification, trained on publicly available spaceflight urine biomarker datasets from NASA's Open Science Data Repository, including OSD-656 and OSD-571 (Inspiration4 mission multiplex biomarker panels). The team will also pursue access to the LSAH astronaut urine chemistry dataset (1,517 samples from 581 astronauts covering calcium, oxalate, citrate, magnesium, uric acid, sulfate, phosphate, sodium, potassium, pH, and urine volume), which represents the primary target corpus for model validation. As additional training data becomes available and the target analyte set expands, the team will evaluate Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and Transformer architectures to capture temporal patterns in longitudinal biomarker sequences. The goal is to scale from binary classification (healthy vs. at-risk) to multi-class health assessments covering conditions detectable through urinalysis, such as renal stone risk, dehydration, and bone mineral loss. The final architecture selection will be driven by classification performance on available astronaut-relevant datasets. |

*[Hardware design, fabrication, and sensor development portions — to be completed by hardware team]*

**ML/Data Work Stream (Computation Team — James):** The ML team begins with a structured learning period to build team competency in neural network architectures (MLP, CNN, Transformer) and biomedical data analysis. In parallel, the team acquires and explores publicly available spaceflight urine biomarker datasets from NASA's Open Science Data Repository, beginning with OSD-656 and OSD-571 (Inspiration4 mission). These datasets are used to build the data ingestion and preprocessing pipeline: cleaning, normalization, feature engineering (e.g., analyte ratios such as calcium/creatinine), and baseline profiling per subject. The team also initiates a formal data access request for the LSAH astronaut urine chemistry dataset (1,517 samples, 581 astronauts) through the NASA Life Sciences Portal, as this controlled-access dataset represents the primary training corpus for model development in Phase 2. **Decision gate:** A functional preprocessing pipeline that produces clean, normalized feature vectors from raw dataset inputs. |
| --- |


| ▸ Work plan narrative — Phase 2 (ML Development & System Integration, Months 5–8)  How will the ML model be trained? What integration tests will be run?

*[Hardware integration and sensor calibration portions — to be completed by hardware team]*

**ML/Data Work Stream (Computation Team — James):** Model development begins with a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) trained for binary health classification (nominal vs. anomalous) using the preprocessed biomarker data. The MLP serves as the baseline architecture. The team then evaluates a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to capture local temporal patterns in longitudinal biomarker sequences, followed by a Transformer architecture to model longer-range dependencies across sampling timepoints. Each architecture is evaluated against a null hypothesis that the model's classifications are no better than random chance, with statistical significance assessed at p < 0.05. If the null hypothesis cannot be rejected for a given architecture, the team revisits feature engineering and data augmentation strategies before proceeding. **Decision gate:** At least one architecture must demonstrate statistically significant classification performance (p < 0.05) on a held-out validation set. |

| ▸ Management structure, methodology, and SME engagement plan

 Project IMULAS will use a hybrid project management approach, which is a phase-gate control for hardware and prototypes, with recurring coordination for software, machine learning, and data activities. Due to their structured, sequential nature, phase gate controls are well-suited for hardware design, fabrication, and validation, whereas machine learning modeling, data analysis, and systems integration are well-suited to an iterative refinement process as results are produced. The PI will be Jai Penumarthi, as he will direct the technical aspects of the project, make major technical scope decisions, and ensure that the technical objectives of the project are aligned with the needs of NASA. Natasha Prabhoo, PM, will coordinate daily team activities, monitor schedules, track readiness of deliverables, monitor risks, and communicate between team members. The projects will consist of three functional work streams: Hardware and Fabrication, Software, Machine Learning and Data, and Systems Integration and Finance. Weekly team meetings and bi-weekly full team reviews will be used to track progress, with written status reports being submitted to Dr. M. Fernanda Mora, NASA SME, in the Chemical Analysis and Life Detection Group at JPL, for both technical review and an external perspective on the project. The PM will coordinate data-to-data execution decisions, and major technical scope decisions and approval of milestones will be made by the PI with input from the appropriate work stream leads and NASA SME. The intent of the proposed management structure is to support the credible execution of IMULAS over the 12-month duration of the project.

| ▸ NASA Strategic alignment — specific goals and roadmap connections  Which NASA Strategic Goals does IMULAS support? Which HRP risks does it address?
IMULAS directly supports several objectives outlined in the 2022 NASA Strategic Plan, particularly within the domains of human spaceflight health monitoring and long-duration exploration missions.
The technology addresses risks identified by NASA’s Human Research Program (HRP), specifically the Risk of Renal Stone Formation during long-duration space missions. Astronauts experience increased calcium excretion and altered fluid balance in microgravity, which can significantly elevate kidney stone risk. Continuous monitoring of urinary biomarkers such as calcium, creatinine, and electrolyte levels can enable early detection and preventative interventions.
Currently, urine health monitoring on the International Space Station relies on manual collection and periodic biochemical analysis, which results in limited measurement frequency and delayed feedback. IMULAS proposes a real-time monitoring system integrated into the ISS Urine Monitoring System (UMS) that can automatically analyze urine biomarkers with minimal crew involvement.
The proposed system aligns with NASA Technology Taxonomy TX06 (Human Health, Life Support, and Habitation Systems) by improving astronaut health monitoring capabilities, and TX11 (Software, Modeling, Simulation, and Information Processing) through the application of machine learning for biomarker interpretation.
IMULAS also supports the operational requirements of Artemis missions and future deep-space habitats, where continuous autonomous health monitoring systems will be critical due to communication delays and limited medical resources. |

Beyond its application in spaceflight, IMULAS has strong dual-use potential for terrestrial healthcare and biomedical monitoring systems.
Continuous urine biomarker analysis could enable advances in point-of-care diagnostics, particularly for kidney disease monitoring, metabolic health assessment, and early detection of electrolyte imbalance. Portable microfluidic diagnostic systems could significantly improve care in remote medical environments, including rural healthcare settings, disaster response, and military field medicine.
The integration of microfluidic sensing with machine learning analytics also represents an emerging field in biomedical technology that can support automated health monitoring platforms for hospitals, wearable health systems, and remote patient monitoring devices.
Additionally, participation in the NPWEE program provides valuable STEM workforce development, exposing students to interdisciplinary fields including biomedical engineering, machine learning, and space systems design. The project therefore contributes not only to NASA mission capabilities but also to the development of a future workforce trained in advanced space technologies and biomedical innovation. |
