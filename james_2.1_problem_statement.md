2.1 Problem Statement & Existing Limitations

Astronaut urine monitoring aboard the International Space Station currently relies on manual sample collection through the Waste and Hygiene Compartment (WHC) and Urine Monitoring System (UMS). Collected samples are either stored for return to Earth or analyzed with limited onboard capabilities. This workflow produces infrequent measurements — typically tied to scheduled medical exams rather than continuous monitoring — and introduces significant latency between sample collection and clinical interpretation. There is no real-time, onboard system capable of analyzing urine biomarkers and providing immediate health status feedback to crew or flight surgeons.

This limitation becomes critical for missions beyond low Earth orbit. Artemis lunar missions and future deep-space exploration will face communication delays of up to 20+ minutes each way, making ground-dependent analysis impractical for time-sensitive health events such as acute kidney stone risk or rapid electrolyte imbalance. Autonomous onboard health monitoring is therefore a prerequisite for sustained human presence beyond LEO.

From a machine learning development standpoint, the data landscape presents a parallel challenge. Publicly available spaceflight urine biomarker datasets are extremely limited in scale. The most accessible datasets — OSD-656 and OSD-571 from the Inspiration4 mission — contain data from only 4 crew members across 9 timepoints, which is insufficient for training supervised classification models with meaningful accuracy. The only dataset with adequate sample size for ML development is the LSAH astronaut urine chemistry dataset (1,517 samples from 581 astronauts covering calcium, oxalate, citrate, magnesium, uric acid, sulfate, phosphate, sodium, potassium, pH, and urine volume), but this dataset requires formal controlled-access approval through NASA with an estimated lead time of 4–6 months. Other listed datasets have proven inaccessible: OSD-343 returns a restricted-access error, and OSD-586 could not be located in public repositories.

Additionally, existing monitoring approaches do not capture longitudinal biomarker trajectories on a per-astronaut basis. Astronaut health assessment is fundamentally a change-detection problem — deviations from an individual's pre-flight baseline are more clinically meaningful than absolute values compared to population norms. Current data collection practices do not support the continuous, individualized monitoring needed for this type of analysis.

IMULAS addresses these gaps by proposing an integrated microfluidic and machine learning system that performs real-time, onboard urine biomarker analysis with automated health classification, eliminating dependence on ground-based data links and enabling continuous autonomous health monitoring for current and future exploration missions.
